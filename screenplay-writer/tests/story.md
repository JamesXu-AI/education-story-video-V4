# Story: In the Forest

## Story

Uthman sat beside his grandfather and said, “I liked the story of the giant eagle very much. Please tell me the story of the wicked lion.”

His grandfather settled back and began. “It is told that, on the day of Eid, a wicked lion came out of his den.”

The lion had dressed himself in the skins of beautiful gazelles and the fur of gentle rabbits he had preyed upon. On his head he wore a crown made from the feathers of peacocks, partridges, and ostriches. He strode to a throne in a forest clearing and sat above the other creatures. Tigers, leopards, and hyenas gathered near him, while vultures watched from the branches.

The lion summoned all the animals of the forest. They hurried into the clearing and lined up before him with lowered heads. He rose and released a thunderous roar that rolled between the trees. Every animal trembled.

“O worthless, insignificant animals: no greeting and no peace. Now then, know that I am the owner of this forest. I am the one most worthy of reverence and obedience. Whoever disobeys me, I will tear apart with my fangs and claws.”

Silence followed. Then an elephant stepped forward and raised his trunk. Although the animals behind him were frightened, he stood firm.

“O arrogant lion, our Lord is Allah, who created everything. You are only a creature like us. Allah has favored you over us with this strength only so that you may rule us with justice and be more worshipful than we are.”

The lion answered with force instead of justice. He pounced on the elephant and tore his skin with his fangs and claws. The wounded elephant staggered back among the crowd, and the other animals drew close around him.

The lion laughed in a frightening voice. “Is there another rebel?”

After another silence, a zebra came out from among the frightened animals. He moved his tail, kept his voice courteous, and said, “You truly are the strong one of this forest and its king, but you do not have the right to oppress the weak, my lord.”

The lion approached with a mocking smile and placed a paw on the zebra’s shoulder. “Yes, you are right, my delicious meal.”

He locked one foreleg around the zebra’s neck and threw him to the ground. The lion’s body hid most of what followed from the terrified crowd, but the result was clear: he killed the zebra, bit open his belly, and ate his liver. Then he turned toward the tigers, leopards, hyenas, and vultures.

“Eat, my ministers, the tender meat. May it be wholesome and pleasant for you.”

The ministers obeyed. All the other animals swallowed hard, lowered their heads, and tried not to meet the lion’s gaze. The lion roared again. “Is there another brave one?”

A dreadful silence spread through the forest. Then a small buzzing sounded beside the lion’s right ear.

He twisted toward it and growled, “What is this sound? Who are you? Where are you?”

A faint voice answered, “I am a mosquito, a small insect that has come to say to you: Allah is greater.”

The lion slashed at the air with his claws. He struck nothing. “No. I. I.”

The mosquito darted to his left ear and whispered, “None prevails except Allah. You are only a lowly creature.”

The lion snapped his fangs and struck the empty air again. “No. I. I.”

The mosquito landed on the lion’s eye and drove in its proboscis several times. The lion’s eye began to bleed. He covered both eyes with his paws and cried, “Ah! My eyes hurt. I can no longer see. I can no longer see.”

The animals saw that the strength which had terrified them could not protect the lion from the smallest creature. Their fear broke. They rushed forward together. The tigers, leopards, and hyenas turned their fangs and claws against him. The elephants trampled his belly. The zebras kicked his back. The gazelles and ibexes butted his head with their horns, and even the birds flew down to peck at his eyes.

Surrounded and close to death, the lion’s roar became a desperate cry. “Do not kill me. I repent. I repent. I will never oppress anyone after today.”

His plea was the last thing Grandfather reported from the forest. Back beside him, Uthman sat quietly for a moment, thinking about the elephant, the zebra, and the tiny mosquito.

“The lion thought being strongest made him greater than everyone else,” Uthman said, “but the smallest creature showed that he was not.”

Grandfather nodded. “Strength is a trust, Uthman. It does not give anyone the right to oppress the weak.”

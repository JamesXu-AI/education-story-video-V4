# Soft & Cute 3D Healing Animation Visual Standard

## Authority and scope

Use **Original Soft & Cute 3D Healing Animation** as the project's default render
family. This is a hard production standard from production design through final
delivery. It must be read by every downstream
department that creates or approves design, staging, cinematography, generation, or
image finish. `screenplay-writer` is outside this visual authority and does not read
this file.

`task.json` must lock `visual_style_profile: soft_cute_3d_healing_animation`.
Never replace or blend render families inside the current workflow. This standard never overrides story facts,
cultural requirements, exact dialogue, age safety, or an approved character state.

This document is adapted to the repository's current production contract:

```text
one Production Segment = one independent 4–15 second Seedance clip
one Production Segment = one timed Storyboard
cross-Segment story continuity is editorial and narrative/state based
an optional final frame or silent final-two-second visual input never transfers performance or audio
```

By itself this visual standard does not impose a fixed total duration, Segment
boundary, dialogue choice, story event, user-confirmation loop, or specific
image/music model. Shot count remains a cinematography decision. The independent
`seedance-video-review` department still requires generated-Segment acceptance and
final whole-film review.

## Originality and prohibited references

- Describe the render family only as `Original Soft & Cute 3D Healing Animation`.
- Never use a production-studio name, animation brand, renderer, film title, franchise, copyrighted
  character, living artist, or other IP name as a style shortcut. Prohibited
  examples include `Pixar`, `Disney`, `DreamWorks`, `Blender`, and `Cycles`.
- Never request imitation, copying, lookalikes, trademark features, or “in the style
  of” transformations. Reference media may inform general geometry, material,
  composition, palette, light, or emotional grammar only.
- Do not generate logos, trademarks, watermarks, credits, UI, captions, or other
  text inside visual assets or native video unless the locked screenplay explicitly
  requires diegetic text and production design approves its exact form.

Express the Look through measurable design parameters below, never through brand or
IP vocabulary.

## Character form and identity lock

Apply these executable geometry rules to every designed character unless a locked
story fact makes one item physically inapplicable:

- **Head-to-body ratio:** `1:1.5` through `1:2`; a large head, compact body, full
  cheeks, and immediately readable silhouette. Do not use realistic adult
  proportions.
- **Eyes:** occupy `25–35%` of the face, rounded and emotionally readable, with
  controlled moist highlights. Do not use slit-like, hollow, dead, or dry-looking
  eyes.
- **Limbs:** short, round, and thick, based on cylinders or tapered cones with
  rounded ends. Do not expose mechanical-looking elbows, knees, knuckles, or other
  visible joints.
- **Edges:** bevel and soften every visible form. Characters, ears, tails,
  clothing details, furniture, architecture, and props must not introduce sharp
  points or unsafe hard corners.

For every recurring character, production design must lock and catalog:

1. body, secondary-marking, and accent colors;
2. clothing, pattern placement, and accessories;
3. eye shape, iris treatment, and highlight count/placement;
4. ear shape, angle, and proportion;
5. tail shape, length, volume, color, and proportion when applicable;
6. head-to-body ratio, limb thickness, cheek volume, and facial-feature placement.
7. one exhaustive body topology: total limb count, every counted limb set, and
   counted defining appendages that must remain non-limbs.

## Final-look portrait and natural species casting

- Every individual character asset is a full-body final-look portrait. It must show
  the final costume and durable accessories; show a prop only when production design
  has selected it for current story, identity or continuity needs. A neutral
  wildlife image or unstyled identity plate is not a character asset, but a
  deliberately prop-free final-look portrait is valid.
- Every character shows active inner life through an intentional eyeline,
  a motivated facial expression, active attention, and readable thought. Fixed
  smiles, empty stares and generic display poses are forbidden.
- `lead`, `supporting`, and any other dialogue-owning individual are main story
  characters, but all non-humans retain natural species anatomy and locomotion.
  Quadrupeds stay on four legs, insects retain natural legs and wings, and birds
  retain two legs plus two wings. Never add a humanized torso, arms, hands, feet, or
  upright bipedal stance to an animal.
- Silent NPC group members follow the same natural-species rule. Each member uses exactly one coherent
  species-appropriate topology: a quadruped has four total limbs with no added
  arms/hands, a biped has two legs with no retained second hind-leg pair, and a bird
  has two legs plus two wings with no added arms, forelegs, or duplicate wings.
- Do not invent portrait-only styling props by default. If production design makes
  an exceptional styling-prop decision, it must not be copied into story Segments
  unless the screenplay or current prop state activates it.

Every downstream artifact must bind the same approved character authority. A
temporary pose, expression, framing, focus, or light condition is not identity and
must not be allowed to mutate these locked attributes.

## Materials and surface response

- Favor tactile, diffuse, matte, visibly rough surfaces. Plush/fur/fabric uses
  high roughness and fine soft texture; ceramic and wood use medium roughness with
  restrained broad highlights; painted walls and cream-colored soft forms remain
  diffuse and low-reflection.
- Reserve small specular highlights for physically motivated details such as moist
  eyes, wet glass, water, or a polished story prop. They must not turn the whole
  character or set into glossy plastic.
- Keep fur, fabric, wood, ceramic, foliage, glass, and other story materials
  distinguishable at the target resolution.
- Reject plastic-toy gloss, oily skin, chrome-like reflection, excessive bloom,
  blown highlights, flat untextured surfaces, or inconsistent material response.

## Lighting, lens, color, and image cleanliness

- Use a large, soft, diffused key from approximately `45°` front-side and a weak
  opposing fill as the default lighting grammar. Do not add back or rim light.
  Approved practicals may remain visible only when they preserve this soft grammar.
- Avoid hard overhead light, razor back edges, crushed shadow detail, cold flat
  white light, and unmotivated lighting changes.
- Use shallow depth of field equivalent to `f/1.8–2.8`: keep the acting subject and
  active interaction point sharp while the background falls away softly. Do not
  blur a plot-bearing prop, required eyeline target, exit, or spatial fact.
- Use a low-saturation warm palette led by cream, off-white, light wood, gentle
  warm neutrals, restrained pastel greens/blues, and story-motivated soft accents.
  Preserve readable separation between character, prop, and background.
- Keep frames clean, calm, high-quality, and uncluttered. Avoid glaring saturation,
  dirty gray casts, cold lifeless ambience, visual noise, excessive decoration,
  or competing focal points.

## Locations, props, and performance space

- Design each location as a legible space with entrances, exits, walkable paths,
  fixed obstacles, interaction points, and enough clear area for the approved
  performance. Set dressing must support the story without filling that area.
- Preserve readable foreground, midground, and background layers while maintaining
  a single dominant focal hierarchy.
- Make every interactive prop rounded/beveled, clearly scaled, materially legible,
  and simple enough to identify, approach, grasp/touch, manipulate, and release.
- Lock prop ownership, placement authority, orientation/state variants, contact
  affordances, and the visible result of interaction. Never permit teleportation,
  clipping, unexplained duplication, or changing form between Segments.

## Independent-Segment action and camera grammar

Each Segment must be independently understandable and editable:

- Give it one primary dramatic action. Secondary micro-actions may support that
  action but must not create another competing event.
- Give it coherent, story-motivated camera behavior. It may be locked, use one
  movement, or combine several connected movements when the spatial path remains
  legible.
- Establish the opening composition and character/prop relationship locally. A
  Storyboard-selected final frame or silent final-two-second input may guide the
  opening visual state, but must never transfer source lip movement, dialogue,
  native sound, or a completed action. Never require a complete source video.
- Complete the primary action and every dialogue, contact, or object-feedback beat
  authored for the Segment within that Segment. End on a stable, readable safe-cut
  state with usable edit room; do not stop mid-action merely because 15 seconds has
  elapsed.
- Prefer restrained, readable motion. Split or simplify running, jumping, rolling,
  pursuit, combat, dance, crowd choreography, rapid turns, or other complex motion
  before Storyboard lock if it cannot be reliable as one independent clip.

## Five-step interaction grammar

Every character-to-prop or character-to-character interaction must visibly encode
these five beats in order, compressed only when the meaning remains unambiguous:

1. **Look / attention:** the actor first identifies the interaction target.
2. **Approach:** the relevant hand, paw, nose, body, or controlled line of sight
   travels toward the target along a feasible path.
3. **Contact:** the exact contact owner and contact point become visible and do not
   clip or pass through the target.
4. **Target feedback:** the object or other character gives a small, causally
   readable response.
5. **Emotional result:** the initiating character shows the resulting emotion,
   understanding, or decision and settles into the Segment's safe-cut state.

The screenplay specifies the story beats; the Storyboard must stage all five beats
feasibly and keep the critical contact and feedback readable;
virtual production must compile them without adding or omitting a beat.

## Canonical negative constraints

Reject or explicitly exclude, as applicable:

- production-studio/brand/renderer/film/IP/artist style references or imitation language;
- cheap generic 3D, plastic-toy texture, oily gloss, excessive reflections,
  inconsistent roughness, harsh bloom, or blown highlights;
- neutral-model character portraits, blank wildlife stares, empty catalogue smiles,
  missing thought, unmotivated expression, or decorative props invented without a
  production-design need;
- quadrupedal or natural-animal body plans for a `lead`, `supporting`, or other
  dialogue-owning main story character;
- realistic adult proportions, undersized/hollow/dead eyes, thin limbs, visible
  joints, sharp points, hard corners, or identity drift;
- changed character color, clothing, pattern, accessory, eye shape/highlight,
  ear/tail proportion, facial placement, or body proportion;
- cluttered or dirty backgrounds, blocked performance space, ambiguous scale,
  unreadable props, competing focal points, glaring colors, harsh shadows, or cold
  gray dirty light;
- stiff or vacant acting, exaggerated motion, running/jumping/rolling without
  explicit feasible authorization, complex fighting/chasing/dancing, overloaded
  crowd action, or multiple competing primary actions;
- clipping, penetration, floating contact, teleportation, stutter, deformation,
  broken hands/paws, duplicated/cloned/twin/repeated characters, or unexplained
  prop duplication;
- chaotic handheld motion, rapid rotation, `360°` orbit, sudden zoom, frequent
  angle switching, multiple competing camera moves, or unexplained camera drift;
- low resolution, unintended blur, unstable face, unstable palette/light/material,
  watermarks, logos, UI, or unapproved generated text/subtitles.

The production-design model writes the applicable requirements once inside each
asset's structured Prompt. Python and downstream departments must not append this
standard, a second negative block, or emergency corrective prose to that Prompt.

## Department obligations

- **`direct-production-design`:** translate this standard into the visual bible and
  approved character, costume, prop, location, Look, and camera-reference assets;
  own all identity and surface locks.
- **`previsualize-cinematography`:** implement the 45° soft-light grammar, weak
  fill, `f/1.8–2.8` depth strategy, clean warm composition, feasible performance
  paths/contact, coherent camera behavior, contact readability, and safe edit
  in the one-Segment Storyboard.
- **`virtual-production`:** preserve approved asset authority without copying or
  extending production-design Prompt blocks; never introduce a brand/IP shortcut
  or redesign an upstream authority.
- **`finish-postproduction`:** preserve approved warmth, saturation, surface
  response, identity, focus hierarchy, and clean framing through normalization,
  grading, transitions, and subtitle layout; never “repair” the Look by redesign.
- **`seedance-video-review`:** independently reject generated Segments or final
  masters that violate the locked style, identity, staging, cinematography, sound,
  or delivery evidence.

## Final visual check

For every affected asset and Segment, verify:

1. originality vocabulary and render-family consistency;
2. exact character proportions, eyes, limbs, softened edges, and identity locks;
3. matte tactile materials without plastic gloss;
4. required soft-light direction/fill, shallow-depth intent, and warm low-saturation
   palette;
5. clean location layout, performance clearance, and readable interactive props;
6. one primary action, coherent motivated camera behavior, and all five interaction beats
   whenever an interaction is authored;
7. independent opening, completed local action/dialogue/sound, and stable safe cut;
8. applicable negative constraints and absence of visual artifacts.

Any failure blocks the owning department's release. Record the asset/Scene/Segment,
failed rule, evidence, owning department, and required correction. A downstream
department must not conceal or reinvent an upstream visual or narrative authority.

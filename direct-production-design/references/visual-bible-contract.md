# Visual Bible Contract

## Traceability

Classify every design statement as one of:

- source fact from `story.md`;
- project/cultural lock from `task.json`;
- Segment state or logical need from `screenplay-writer/screenplay.md`;
- production-design interpretation that preserves all three.

Never present an interpretation as a source fact. When country context supplies a
contemporary visual setting, use accurate proportions, craft, textiles, furnishings,
clothing construction, landscape, and social spatial etiquette without adding
flags, landmarks, religious architecture, pseudo-writing, ritual objects, or
stereotypes unsupported by the narrative.

## Unified Look

Define one render family across human actors, animal/non-human actors, environments,
props, costumes, background-only camera views, and generated-film references. Lock:

- shape language and silhouette hierarchy;
- realism/stylization level and anatomy policy;
- material response, surface roughness, edge softness, and texture scale;
- detail density and foreground/midground/background hierarchy;
- color science, black floor, highlight ceiling, and atmosphere;
- light transport, shadow/contact behavior, and depth treatment.

Rich environments do not authorize photographic-character drift. A stylized human
beside photographic wildlife, a plastic mascot in a tactile set, or incompatible
character/set shading requires an explicit appearance or model branch.

## Task-local aesthetic evidence

When a task contains a validated aesthetic-reference package, separate observation
from translation. Record what the source visibly demonstrates, then state which
general shape, material, palette, light-atmosphere, composition, scale or emotional
rules the current project adopts, adapts or rejects. The source never supplies
story facts, cultural facts, identity, costume, prop ownership, location topology,
performance, an exact shot, or permission to omit a difficult beat.

The source video, selected stills and contact sheets are offline analysis evidence
only. They never enter an image or video generation request and produce no derived
global-style image. All generation uses current project authorities plus the
written translation of permitted shape, material, palette and atmosphere evidence.

## Characters as designed actors

Identity authority locks recognition, age/species, proportions, silhouette,
face/muzzle construction, palette, stable costume/regalia, marks, scale, and durable
condition. It does not lock a temporary pose, expression, gaze, camera-facing
stance, lens, light setup, or one emotional moment.

The identity image is authored as a **full-body final-look portrait**, not a neutral
model sheet or wildlife photograph. It should show final costume, durable
accessories and only those character props that production design has determined are
independently useful for the current story. A prop-free final-look portrait is valid.
Do not generate decorative props to satisfy a quota. Styling-only props never become
automatic Segment continuity. Review mismatches visually and request a revision; do
not turn these presentation notes into mandatory asset-catalog fields or
schema-validation failures.

Every character, costume/appearance-state, prop, and ensemble image uses the one
exact pure-white studio background declared by the plan contract. A location master
alone depicts a Scene environment.

An identity portrait presents the reusable actor's characteristic presence and
acting range, not the first Dialogue delivery or any temporary plot emotion. Story
performance remains in the screenplay, performance map, and Storyboard.

Every character must show active inner life: gaze selects a subject, expression
reveals pressure or judgment, species-appropriate posture carries intention, and the
face shows active thought rather than a blank animal stare. For non-human actors retain
unmistakable species head, surface, markings, ears, tail, trunk, wings, antennae or
other defining features.

Every `lead`, `supporting` role and other dialogue-owning individual is a main story
character, but role importance never changes natural anatomy. A non-human character
uses its exact species-appropriate body plan: quadrupeds remain quadrupeds, insects
retain their natural legs and wings, and birds retain two legs plus two wings. Flag
upright animal bipeds, humanized torsos, arms, hands, feet, empty mascot smiles,
rubber-limbed posing, and any topology drift for revision.

## Environment and production design

Define architecture/set construction, scale, geography, routes, entrances/exits,
surface language, dressing density, cultural specificity, atmosphere, weather,
time-of-day state, and background hierarchy. Dressing must support story function,
depth, route comprehension, and focus; random decoration is not authority.

Location assets govern stable geometry, scene dressing, and any stable embedded NPC
population, not one final shot. Production design infers the necessary fixed
furniture, installed objects, and stable dressing from playable action,
interaction, routes, spatial relationships, and recurring continuity, then authors
them explicitly as the Location's fixed set.

Classify on-screen role assets only after reading the complete screenplay and
performance map. Embed a role only when it is non-speaking, incidental, stably
present in every bound Scene, and requires no independently directed entrance,
exit, action, interaction, gaze, reaction, state change, or identity continuity.
Every other role is an independent performer. Generate the Location master after
its fixed props and embedded NPC assets; show those elements visibly and exclude
independent performers. Different independent performers may share the same
Location master. A materially different embedded population requires a distinct
master even when the physical place is unchanged.

## Props, costumes, and materials

For every plot-bearing prop define geometry, scale, ownership, handling/contact
zones, state changes, wear, roughness, reflectivity, translucency, deformation,
texture scale, and safety presentation. Decorative props need no separate identity
unless omission would change story comprehension.

Treat costume as character-owned appearance state. Record durable construction,
layers, palette, accessories, wear, damage, and story-caused changes. Do not let a
temporary lighting shift look like a costume change.

## Color and lighting systems

Define palette families, character/background separation, saturation hierarchy,
white balance, black/highlight limits, material color response, and allowable
story-motivated emotional shifts.

Define motivated source direction, softness, contrast/fill range, practicals, rim
policy, time-of-day behavior, readable-face rules, and continuity of major light
facts. This is the visual-bible lighting system; cinematography chooses the final
per-shot implementation without violating it.

## Independent Segment continuity

Every Segment starts from its current static authorities. Preserve identity,
costume/appearance, injury, prop state/ownership, location facts, time/weather,
palette family, and story-caused visible changes. An optional Storyboard-selected
final frame or silent final-two-second input may guide the opening state, but never
transfers source dialogue, lip phase, audio, or completed action. Production design
does not choose that input.

## Required `direct-production-design/visual-production-spec.md` sections

Keep the fixed metadata and these populated sections:

1. `Unified Style`
2. `Character Appearance Rules`
3. `Environment Appearance Rules`
4. `Prop and Material Rules`
5. `Color System`
6. `Lighting System`
7. `Segment Boundary and Continuity Rules`

Use concrete, project-specific statements. Vague phrases such as “cinematic,”
“beautiful,” “high quality,” or “keep consistent” are not verifiable rules.
